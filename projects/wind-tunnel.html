Overview

This project involved the design and construction of a desktop scale wind tunnel to study airflow behavior and compare airfoil geometries. The system was developed as part of a university engineering course focused on experimental analysis and fluid behavior.

Design and Fabrication

The wind tunnel structure and test section were designed for additive manufacturing and fabricated using a 3D printer. The modular design allowed for rapid iteration and easy swapping of airfoil test samples.

Multiple airfoil shapes were modeled and printed to evaluate differences in flow attachment, separation, and wake behavior under the same flow conditions.

Smoke Flow Visualization

To visualize airflow, a custom smoke generation system was designed and integrated upstream of the test section. The smoke system produced a consistent flow of visible tracer particles, allowing qualitative observation of streamlines and boundary layer behavior around each airfoil.

Testing and Results

Airfoil performance was evaluated by observing flow patterns at different angles of attack. The setup made it possible to directly compare how geometry influenced flow separation and stability.

Lessons Learned

This project provided hands on experience with experimental setup, rapid prototyping, and qualitative aerodynamic analysis. It reinforced the relationship between theoretical fluid concepts and real world flow behavior.
